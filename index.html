Introduction & Problem Statement

Egypt faces significant Grand Challenges, including the urgent need to improve alternative energy use and reduce a carbon footprint driven by a 90% reliance on fossil fuels. In response, our Capstone project—themed “Energy, Force, and Power”—aims to solve the inefficiencies and high costs of current centralized power grids.

The Problem

Small-scale renewable systems often fail to utilize inconsistent resources, resulting in unreliable output and low rotational speeds.

The Solution

A Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine (HAWT) equipped with a mechanical gearbox speed multiplier to maximize aerodynamic efficiency.

Design Constraints: Max volume of 0.25 m³ | Min output of 150 Joules in 5 minutes.

Design & Mechanism

Horizontal axis wind turbines (HAWTs) have a rich history dating back to the 12th century, with significant modern improvements established by pioneers like James Blyth and Poul la Cour. For our project, we selected a three-bladed HAWT configuration as the most suitable option for optimizing aerodynamic efficiency, stability, and economic viability.

The length of each blade is fixed at 42 cm to maximize the captured wind power. Despite these capabilities, the prototype maintains a footprint of 110 cm × 70 cm × 20 cm ($0.154\text{ m}^3$), comfortably satisfying our strict volume constraint of $0.25\text{ m}^3$.

Principles & Laws

Figure 7: Airflow visualization showing high and low pressure zones.

Operation is primarily based on Bernoulli’s Principle, where faster-moving air over the curved upper surface creates low pressure, resulting in a lift force ($L$) that drives the rotor. This relationship is defined by:

$$P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho gh_1 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho gh_2$$

To ensure peak performance, we utilize pitch control to maintain an optimal angle of attack between 5° and 8°, maximizing the lift-to-drag ratio.

NACA 0018 Airfoil Analysis

The NACA 0018 airfoil features a symmetric cross-section, allowing for efficient lift generation and stable operation. This geometric precision ensures that the rotor delivers consistent rotational energy to the main shaft.

Gears' Mechanical Advantages

To bridge the gap between blade rotation and generator requirements, we implemented high-efficiency spur gears (up to 99% efficiency). By applying the principle of torque ($\tau = r \times F$), we increased the distance between the blade and shaft to generate more power.

Speed Multiplication:

We utilized a 4:1 gear-tooth ratio. A 48-tooth driving gear fixed on the shaft transfers motion to a 12-tooth driven gear on the generator. Consequently, for every full rotation of the turbine blades, the generator spins four times.

Future Recommendations

As technology advances, we acknowledge that no project is perfect. These recommendations serve as a roadmap for future teams to iterate upon our design and push the boundaries of efficiency.

1 Metallic Gearbox Integration

Metallic gearboxes should be utilized in the turbine assembly to handle significantly higher torque and continuous mechanical loads. Their superior resistance to temperature fluctuations and long-term fatigue makes them essential for a durable, industrial-grade system.

2 Structural Interlocking Stability

By interlocking the turbine tower directly with the base, the structure becomes more stable. This mechanical integration allows the tower to resist higher airspeeds without deflection, ensuring the system remains upright and vibration-free during peak winds.

3 Carbon Fiber Axis Implementation

Transitioning to carbon fiber for the axis connecting the blades and gears offers an ultra-strong, lightweight alternative to traditional metals. Composed of thin crystalline filaments, this material reduces rotational inertia, allowing the turbine to generate power more efficiently at lower wind speeds.

4 Advanced Sensors and Electronics

Integrating smart electronics, such as pitch angle sensors, enables the turbine to identify the most productive angle for the blades in real-time. This active adjustment maximizes the electric current generated across varying weather conditions.

5 Mechanical Braking Pads

Essential for safety, a braking pad system must be included to stop the turbine during extreme events. This allows for critical mechanical repairs and prevents structural damage during storms that exceed the system's operational limits.
